Practitioner Allowable Scope of Practice in Ontario
This is a rich and rapidly evolving topic. Here's a comprehensive reference covering the major practitioner types in Ontario, grounded in the RHPA framework.
The foundational framework: the RHPA and controlled acts
Ontario's scope of practice scheme is set out in the Regulated Health Professions Act, 1991 (RHPA) and consists of two main elements: a scope of practice statement and the controlled acts authorized to each profession. The scope of practice statement is found in each profession-specific Act, while controlled acts are procedures and treatments considered to pose a risk of harm when performed by someone unqualified.
There are 14 controlled acts under the RHPA. These are acts that can only be performed by regulated health professionals whose scope includes authorization to perform the act. The 14 controlled acts include: communicating a diagnosis, performing surgery, setting fractures, prescribing drugs, dispensing drugs, administering substances by injection or inhalation, applying forms of energy (e.g. electricity, MRI), psychotherapy, managing labour and delivery, allergy challenge testing, and several others.
Understanding which practitioner can do what essentially means understanding which of these 14 controlled acts each profession is authorized to perform independently, which require an order or delegation, and which are entirely off-limits.
Physicians (MDs)
Physicians have the broadest scope of any practitioner in Ontario. They are governed by the Medicine Act, 1991 and the RHPA, and regulated by the College of Physicians and Surgeons of Ontario (CPSO).
Physicians are authorized to perform all 14 controlled acts. Practically, this means they can:
Diagnosis and assessment: Conduct comprehensive history-taking, physical examination, and diagnostic workup. Communicate any diagnosis — including serious, complex, or life-threatening conditions — to patients and their representatives.
Prescribing: Prescribe any drug, including controlled substances (narcotics, benzodiazepines, stimulants), off-label medications, and monitored drugs. No restrictions on drug class.
Procedures: Perform surgery (cutting below the dermis), set and cast fractures and dislocations, insert instruments into body orifices, administer substances by injection or inhalation, apply any prescribed form of energy (defibrillation, pacemaker therapy, ECT, MRI ordering, ultrasound).
Mental health: Perform psychotherapy, issue Form 1 (application for psychiatric assessment) and Form 3 (certificate of involuntary admission) under the Mental Health Act, certify incapacity.
Ordering and labs: Order any laboratory test, diagnostic imaging (X-ray, CT, MRI, nuclear medicine), and specialist referrals without restriction.
Certifying and legal: Sign death certificates in all circumstances, complete WSIB/disability forms with full clinical authority, certify fitness to drive (DRIVABLE assessments), complete Form 1 for involuntary psychiatric detention.
Delegation: Physicians are the primary source of delegation authority — they can delegate controlled acts to almost any other regulated or even unregulated provider via direct orders or medical directives.
Nurse Practitioners (NPs)
NPs are the closest thing to physicians in terms of autonomous scope. In Ontario, NPs are authorized to: complete a comprehensive health history and assessment, formulate and communicate a diagnosis (taking a differential diagnosis into consideration), prescribe all medications including controlled drugs and substances, dispense, sell and compound medications, and set and cast fractures and dislocated joints.
NPs are also authorized to consult with and refer directly to specialists.
Key NP authorities in more detail:
Prescribing: Nurse practitioners gained the authority to prescribe controlled substances in 2017 and to order CT and MRI scans in 2022. NPs can prescribe narcotics (with DEA-equivalent registration) and monitored drugs, though fentanyl patches require additional safeguards including pharmacy notification.
Procedures: NPs can perform procedures including pelvic exams, Pap tests, wound debridement, suturing, casting, and joint injections. As of July 1, 2025, NPs can order and apply a defibrillator, order and apply a cardiac pacemaker, and order and perform electrocoagulation (to treat skin conditions and lesions).
Mental health: NPs are authorized to perform psychotherapy and can issue involuntary psychiatric holds in some settings (expansion ongoing).
End of life and legal: Proposed changes allow NPs to certify death in all circumstances. NPs can also admit and discharge patients in hospitals in which they have privileges.
Key limitation vs. physicians: NPs generally cannot perform surgery (the surgical controlled act), cannot certify for MAID independently in all circumstances, and in hospital settings may face institutional credentialing constraints that limit what they can practically do even if legislatively authorized. NPs also do not bill OHIP directly — they are almost always salaried, which affects where and how they practice.
There are four types of NP certificates in Ontario: NP – Primary Health Care, NP – Adult, NP – Paediatric, and NP – Anaesthesia. The anaesthesia NP is a newer category with a very specific scope around perioperative care.
Registered Nurses (RNs)
RNs practice under the Nursing Act, 1991 and are regulated by the College of Nurses of Ontario (CNO). Their scope is substantially narrower than NPs but has expanded significantly since 2023.
